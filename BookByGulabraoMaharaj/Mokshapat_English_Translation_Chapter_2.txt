Chapter 2

।। श्रीमत्सद्गुरु ज्ञानेश्वर माउली समर्थ ।।
श्रीमत्सद्गुरु ज्ञानेश्वर चरणीं । साष्टांग प्रणिपात करूनी ।
द्वितीयाध्याय उभारणीं । प्रौढक्रीडार्थ करितांहे ॥१॥
[
Shrimatsadguru Dnyaneshwar Charani | Sashtang Pranipat Karuni |
Dwitiyadhyaya Ubharani | Praudhakridartha Karitahe ||1||
]
Having performed a full prostration at the feet of the most revered Sadguru Shri Dnyaneshwar, I am now commencing the exposition of the Second Chapter for the purpose of this profound divine play. This chapter is for a mature audiences.

Whatever was explained in the first chapter contains very little actual knowledge of the underlying sentiment (bhava). It focuses more on the technical structure (the 'almanac') of the 'play' and other such details. While that level of sentiment is quite sufficient for the general public, I am writing something more specific here for those who truly wish to become experts in this 'play'—and for those great souls whose devotion has become steady and who can be guided consistently all the way to ultimate liberation (Kaivalya).

In Yoga, it is said that the life-force (Prana) and the mind (Chitta) become steady through physical postures (Asanas). However, one poet has remarked: 'If you do not kill your worldly desires (Asha) and yet continue to sit in a meditative pose, your goal will never be achieved. This is not a feat of physical wrestling or mere exercise. It requires the strong support of the internal sentiment found in self-restraint and moral discipline (Yama-Niyama *1).' This same principle applies to our spiritual 'play.' To play without deep sentiment is merely engaging in excessive entertainment. Therefore, for the sake of the general public, the various types of sentiments were explained in the first chapter.

Now, I shall describe the types of sentiment (Bhava) intended for those great souls who are intense seekers of liberation (Tivra Mumukshu *2). It has been stated at the very beginning: 'One should begin the play with the understanding that whatever object has been taken onto the board to play with is, in fact, myself. *3' This sentiment is of two types: 1. Shābdī-bhāvanā (Verbal/Literal Sentiment) and 2. Ārthī-bhāvanā (Substantial/Meaningful Sentiment *4). However, please note that these terms are used here in a specialized, technical sense.

Shabdī Bhāvanā (Verbal/Literal Sentiment)
One who is unable to practice Arthī Bhāvanā (Substantial Sentiment) should practice Shabdī Bhāvanā. This means that instead of saying, 'This object of mine is small, take it to Brahmaloka (the realm of the Creator),' one should at least use words like, 'I myself am going to Brahmaloka' or 'I have gone to Heaven.' Any other such sentiments should be pulled toward oneself in the form of 'I-ness' (Ahanta) or 'My-ness' (Mamata) *5, and those specific words should be uttered—this is what is called Shabdī Bhāvanā. 
	This means that 'if the mind is unable to perform the inner contemplation, then one must at least utter the words in the prescribed(Vidhiyukt) manner. If one utters words contrary to this, they should be cast into a great hell until twenty-five rounds [cycles] are completed.' This rule applies even to the general public.

Ārthī Bhāvanā (Substantial/Internalized Sentiment)
Now, those individuals who are capable of maintaining constant contemplation within the mind—even without speaking any words—should practice Ārthī Bhāvanā. 'Keeping the flow of mental tendencies (Manovrutti) continuous through deep contemplation is what is called Ārthī Bhāvanā.'

One who practices Shābdī-bhāvanā (verbal sentiment) must gradually transition into Ārthī-bhāvanā (internalized sentiment) *6. This shift may take any amount of time, whether it be a single day or an entire Yuga (age). While those practicing Ārthī-bhāvanā do not have a specific need for Shābdī-bhāvanā, if they happen to lose their inner focus (avadhān) *8, they should use the medium of verbal sentiment to pull themselves back to their center. *7

The following rules apply equally to both types of sentiment (Bhāvanā). The only difference is that those with a slow or beginner-level temperament (Mandā) must observe these rules through spoken words, while intense seekers (Tīvra) must observe them through internal contemplation. There are five general types of these sentiments, within which many sub-categories exist.
The Five Types of Sentiments:
1. Satisfaction (Trūpti)
2. Fearlessness (Nirbhayatā)
3. Detachment/Indifference (Upekshā)
4. Fear (Bhīti)
5. Repentance (Pashchāttāp)

1. Upon reaching the state of Kaivalya (absolute liberation) *9, one should maintain the sentiment of Satisfaction (Trupti).
2. In the various realms of liberation—such as the Paryanka *11 (couch) of Brahmaloka, the realms above the Serpent in Paramloka, the realm of Umapati (Shiva), and Goloka (Krishna's realm)—as well as in the trans-worldly realms (Aloka *12), one should maintain the sentiment of Fearlessness (Nirbhaya). In short: be satisfied at the primary/ultimate station, and remain fearless in the secondary *10 stations of liberation.
3. In places of uncertainty (doubtful stations), one should maintain a sentiment of Detachment or Indifference (Upekshā).
4. In places of worldly enjoyment (Bhoga), one should maintain a sentiment of Fear (Bhīti). *14
5. In places of suffering (Dukha), one should maintain a sentiment of Repentance (Pashchāttāp). *15

The stations for Satisfaction (Trūpti) and Fearlessness (Nirbhaya) have already been mentioned above.
The five "Uncertain" (Sandigdha) stations are: Pārameshthya, the Ocean of Milk (Kshīrābdhi), Vishnuloka, Pashupatiloka, and Umaloka. From these realms, one may either fall back [to lower births] or may not fall at all. *13

Since the rule of the Pārameshthya (the highest realm) applies to all four of these worlds, they have been described as being equal to one another.

One should view heavenly realms and other worlds attained through merit only as temporary places of enjoyment, and should maintain a fear of eventually falling from them.

In the same vein, regarding the painful realms of sin, one should live in a constant state of repentance.

This explains the general nature of these places. However, depending on the specific realm, one should cultivate as many focused contemplations as possible, strictly following these four types of sentiments.

Until you attain liberation (Kaivalya), you must rely solely on the help of the Supreme Lord across all states of contemplation. I shall now provide further examples of how these specific contemplations should be practiced.

If I find myself in a hellish realm, I should cultivate a sentiment of repentance: 'O Lord, I am in this hell because of the sins I committed; I shall never commit such sins again. Deliver me from this place quickly.'

Similarly, if by some merit I ascend to heaven, I should think: 'O Lord, there are many paths here that lead back to a downfall. When will You liberate me from such temporary places of enjoyment? *16
Furthermore, if one succeeds in practices such as receiving a share of sacrificial offerings (Yajna-havirbhaga-grahana *17), those contemplations should also be performed right here.

Similarly, if one reaches a state of Yama and Niyama *1 (ethical restraints and observances), one should cultivate sentiments accordingly—focusing the mind on non-violence (Ahimsa), truth (Satya), and so on.
Upon reaching a state of fearlessness, one should reflect: 'O Lord, it is only through Your help that I have arrived here and become fearless; now, I must perform some selfless service (Paropkar) for others.' Such are the sentiments one should cultivate.

In a state of indifference or neglect (upekshya *18), one should cultivate the sentiment: 'O Lord, I am entirely under Your authority *19. Do with me as You will.'
If, despite maintaining good thoughts, one happens to fall into the company of wicked people or undesirable circumstances, one should accept it as the fruit of one's own past actions (karma *20).

Likewise, whatever realm you enter, consider it the result of the merit (Punya) you have earned to deserve being there. However, you must never allow pride in that merit to take root *21.
Since one’s focus on the knowledge of these realms *23 can be easily maintained by following the general rules and exceptions (Utsarga-Apavada *22) discussed in the first chapter, there is no need to establish a separate set of rules for it.

In summary, among the Sattvic, Rajasic, and Tamasic realms: one should enthusiastically practice pure, virtuous sentiments (Sattvic) while in Sattvic realms. In Rajasic realms, one should maintain a sense of caution or fear regarding those passionate impulses. And in Tamasic realms, one should dwell in a state of repentance.

Cultivate pure, spiritual intentions among those beyond the ordinary world(Aloka). That is, maintain the constant realization: 'I am Brahman.' *24

In the state of liberation (Kaivalya), keep this awareness alive: 'I am indeed Brahman; why should I even need to say I am?' *24

The Qualified Seeker (Adhikari):

One who is firmly convinced that even if the perception of the image on the board is false, the underlying principles and sentiments within it are absolutely real—only such a person is qualified for this 'game.'

Others should not be permitted to play.

And for one who develops the desire to play, the Guru—as the instructor of the game—should explain the essential points one by one, gain the seeker's agreement on them, and only then allow the play to commence.


The Four Essential Connections (Anubandha Chatushtaya): *25
The 'qualified seeker' ("Adhikari") mentioned above; the relationship between sentiments and the realms, where one is the seeker and the other the sought ("Sambandha"); the ability to cultivate feelings through spiritual practices and to gain knowledge of those worlds ("Vishaya)"; and the purpose of constantly increasing and firmly maintaining that final feeling of liberation- Kaivalya throughout one's life ("Prayojana")—in this way, the fourfold connection is established. This is the method of the Sankhya, which is the path of Vedanta.

	Sentiments According to Yoga
Now, I shall explain the method of cultivating sentiments through the path of Yoga.
In these sentiments, as well, one should recognize the distinctions between the literal (verbal) and the meaningful (essential). In Yoga, there are primarily eight such sentiments to be considered.

1. While in Bhurloka (the earthly realm), one should focus the mind on the Adhara (Muladhara) Chakra within the body.
2. While in Bhurloka, one should meditate upon the Svadhishthana Chakra within the body.
3. While in Svarloka, meditate on the Manipura.
4. While in Maharloka, meditate on the Anahata Chakra.
5. While in Janaloka, meditate on the Vishuddha Chakra.
6. While in Tapoloka, meditate on the Dvidal (Ajna) Chakra.
7. While in Brahmaloka, meditate on the Sahasradal (Sahasrara).
8. In Aloka, apart from liberation (Kaivalya), one should meditate on the Viveka (discernment) that the Purusha (Pure Consciousness) is distinct from Prakriti (Matter/Nature).

These locations prescribed for the Yogi should be understood as existing within the body itself, from the Kandhasthana (the root/base) to the Shikhasthana (the crown/crest). The only difference is that the focus of the mind shifts from the image on the [game] board to the 'images' (energy centers) within the body. If one falters (experiences a 'fall') while practicing these Yogic sentiments, it should be considered the fruit of Siddhi-bhoga (the enjoyment of supernatural powers). However, in the state of Kaivalya (liberation), the sentiment remains identical for both Vedantins and Yogis.

Having explained the sentiments of the path of Yoga, I shall now describe the sentiments within the path of Karma (Action).


Sentiments of the Path of Karma (Karma-marga)
In the sentiments of the Path of Karma, one does not practice meditation (Dhyana). Instead, one simply acquires the knowledge of where those various realms (Lokas) are located within the body and culminates that understanding in the Virad Upasana (worship of the Cosmic Form). *26 *27
In this path as well, there are two distinctions: the literal (Shabdi) and the meaningful (Arthi).

1. Imagine a Virat Purusha (the Cosmic Being) and, while in the Bhurloka (the earthly realm), envision yourself as a tiny reflection or miniature image of Him.
2. Furthermore, while in Bhurloka, cultivate the visualization that you are situated at the feet of the Virat. *28 *29
3. In Bhuvarloka, visualize yourself at the knees of the Virat.
4. In Svarloka, at the waist.
5. In Maharloka, at the navel.
6. In Janaloka, at the heart.
7. In Tapoloka, at the throat.
8. In the realms of Brahma, Vishnu, Shiva, Maya, and the Supreme (Parama) worlds, cultivate the feeling of being at the forehead (Lalata).
9. In the realms of Aloka (excluding Kaivalya), cultivate the feeling of being at the head (Shirsha); and in Kaivalya, once again, the sentiment is the same for all.

In this manner, all the sentiments (Bhavas) have been described. Their variations are numerous; one should learn them from the respective scriptures and cultivate as many of them as possible.


The Objective:
The purpose of creating this 'game' is to ensure that the supreme sentiment of Kaivalya (liberation) becomes so firm that it lasts a lifetime—or at the very least, through the habit of playing for many days, that same sentiment arises at the moment of death. *30
We clearly state that by playing with these sentiments, one attains those specific realms, even with mild intensity (Mridu-samvega); this fact is established in the Yoga Vasistha *32, thus ensuring a definitive Vedantic benefit. If the intensity becomes moderate (Madhya-samvega), the experience occurs in dreams, and if the intensity is intense (Tivra-samvega), direct physical attainment is entirely natural. *31
This chapter is indeed somewhat difficult, yet anyone who acts as the leader of the 'game' must absolutely understand it. Alternatively, one should at least keep this book open while playing. There must be a spectator—someone who is well-versed in the game. The authority to correct and penalize anyone whose sentiment (Bhava) falters must rest solely with them. *32
Even one who practices the Arthi-bhava (internalized meaning) should speak their sentiment aloud so the spectator can monitor it. If such a spectator is unavailable, a person who has attained Kaivalya (liberation) should be appointed as the observer. If even that is not possible, then within the framework of the board itself, Brahmadeva (Lord Brahma) should be envisioned as the spectator. *34


The Attainment of Results (Phala-prapti)
When one has no other work or the task of reading scriptures is complete, one should play [this game] with as much sentiment (Bhava) as possible for as long as desired. Then, observe what wonders occur in your dreams at night—but your mind must be fully absorbed in the play.
As these pure (Sattvic) dreams begin to increase, then, as explained in the commentary on Patanjali Swami’s Yoga Sutra, 'Svapna-nidra-jnana-alambanam va' (or by relying on the knowledge of dreams and sleep), and specifically the 'Svapna-jnana-alamban' (relying on dream-knowledge) portion of the Vyasa Bhashya, Yogic perfection (Siddhi) will be achieved and Kaivalya (liberation) will be attained. This is a fact proven by authoritative evidence. *35

If such [ample] time is not available, then one should make it a rule to play at least twice, or even just once, every eight days. *36
In this way, this 'game' has undoubtedly become a specialized method of practice (Prakriya-marga *37) within Vedanta. A notable feature of this game is that it does not discard the Guru-Shishya (Teacher-Disciple) tradition *38.

Rule: 
One should not leave the game unless there is an absolutely essential task to attend to. *39

Exceptions:
If there is a difficulty in attaining the state of Brahma *40, an expert player (a person of high character) should assist by making a 'donation' or casting the move on the board for the seeker. One must avoid any Tamasic (ignorant or lazy) *41 actions that could corrupt the pure sentiments (Bhavas). Once everyone has reached Moksha (liberation), if one person remains, they should perform acts of Paropkar (selfless service to others) *42.

Whatever has been said here, may all of it be offered at the holy feet of the illustrious Satguru Jnaneshwar Maharaj.
May this be sufficient.
Victory to the illustrious Satguru, the eternally successful Shri Jnaneshwar Maharaj!


Note: Some may mistake our game for the Trikaldarshi Arsha (the mirror that sees past, present, and future). However, there is a fundamental difference between the two. 
In the case of the Trikaldarshi Arsha, one must first master Yogic sentiments and then look into the mirror—this means moving from Chaitanya (Pure Consciousness) back into Jada (Inert Matter) to perceive the deities. This method is fundamentally strange, as no religion teaches the path of descending from Consciousness back into Matter.
Our game is exactly the opposite. It provides a Sanatana (eternal) and Sulabh (easy) path to ascend from this drawn, material board (Jada-chitrapata) into Chaitanya by cultivating Yogic sentiments *43. This method aligns with the ancient tradition of worshiping sacred idols or the Sarvatobhadra Mandala *44. It is evident that this approach is fully endorsed by the ancient saints who created similar spiritual games in the past.




References:
1. Yama-Niyama (यम-नियम): These are the first two limbs of Ashtanga Yoga, representing ethical rules (like non-violence and truth) and personal disciplines (like purity and contentment). Yamas (restraints) include Ahimsa and Satya, while Niyamas (observances) include purity and contentment.
2. Intense Seekers of Liberation (तीव्र मुमुक्षु): Mumukshu refers to someone with a burning desire for spiritual freedom (Moksha). The "intensity" implies they are beyond casual curiosity.
3. The Object is 'Me' (ती वस्तू 'मी आहे'): This refers to the non-dualistic (Advaita) approach where the seeker dissolves the gap between themselves and the object of their meditation or practice.
4. Shābdī vs. Ārthī Bhāvanā: In traditional Indian philosophy (specifically Mimamsa), these refer to the power of words to inspire action versus the internal purpose/result. Here, the author warns they are technical terms (पारिभाषिक), meaning they carry a specific meaning unique to this particular spiritual "play" or method.
5. Ahanta & Mamata (अहंता व ममता): These refer to the ego-sense ("I") and the sense of possession ("Mine"). The author suggests redirecting these powerful human instincts away from material objects and toward spiritual realities.
6. The Spiritual Ladder: Verbal practice is a stepping stone. The goal is to move from outward speech to inward realization, regardless of how long the process takes.
7.Safety Net: Even for advanced practitioners (those in Ārthī), verbal affirmations act as a "backup" or "reset button" to regain concentration when the mind wanders.
8. Focus (Avadhān): The text emphasizes that the most critical element is keeping one's attention fixed; words are simply the tool to maintain or restore that attention.
9. Kaivalya (कैवल्य): The highest state of liberation where the soul is completely isolated from matter and unified with the Absolute. This is the "Primary Station" where the seeker is "Full" or "Satisfied."
10. Secondary Realms (गौणमुक्ति): These include Brahmaloka, Goloka, and Shivaloka. In Indian philosophy, these are considered "qualified" liberation where some form or duality still exists. The instruction is to remain Fearless here, as one is safe but not yet at the final destination.
11. Paryanka (पर्यंक): Literally a "bed" or "couch," referring to the symbolic throne of the Divine in various heavens.
12. Aloka (अलोक): The vast regions beyond the manifest universe.
13. Sandigdha (संदिग्ध): These are "middle-ground" heavens. The risk is that the seeker might get stuck or return to the cycle of rebirth after their merit is exhausted. Therefore, the advice is Upekshā—do not get too attached to the pleasures there; remain indifferent so you can move higher.
	The Five Uncertain Realms: Even high celestial realms like Vishnuloka or the Ocean of Milk are considered "uncertain" compared to the absolute stability of Kaivalya (liberation), because there is still a subtle thread of duality or potential return.
14. Fear in Enjoyment (भोगस्थानी भीती): This is a profound psychological instruction. The author suggests being "afraid" of worldly pleasures because they distract the seeker and lead to further attachment and eventual downfall.
15. Repentance in Suffering (दुःखस्थानी पश्चात्ताप): Instead of complaining, one should use suffering as a moment of reflection and repentance to cleanse past karmas.
16. Path of Downfall (पतनाचे मार्ग): In Hindu philosophy, Svarga (Heaven) is a temporary destination. Once the merit of your good deeds is exhausted, you "fall" back into the cycle of rebirth on Earth.
17. Yajna-havirbhaga-grahana (यज्ञहविर्भागग्रहण): This refers to the ritual act of accepting the sacrificial portion (havis) offered during a Yajna. In this context, it refers to the mental or spiritual focus one should maintain even during successful religious rituals.
18. Upekshya (उपेक्ष्य): In this philosophical context, it refers to equanimity or a neutral state toward things that are neither helpful nor harmful, or toward people who are indifferent to spiritual life.
19. Authority (अखत्यार): This implies total surrender (Sharanagati) to the divine will, acknowledging that one is not the ultimate "doer."
20. Fruit of Karma (कर्माचें फल): This provides a stoic framework for dealing with misfortune without bitterness, attributing it to past cause-and-effect rather than divine injustice.
21. Pride in Merit (पुण्याचा अभिमान): A warning that spiritual ego—feeling superior because of one's good deeds—can be a trap that halts further progress.
22. Utsarga and Apavada (उत्सर्गापवाद): These are traditional Sanskrit hermeneutic terms. Utsarga refers to a general rule, while Apavada refers to an exception or a specific modification of that rule.
23. Lokajnana (लोकज्ञान): Knowledge regarding the various planes of existence or realms.
24. The philosophy behind these stages of realization is rooted in Advaita Vedanta (non-dualism), which teaches that the individual soul (Atman) and the universal reality (Brahman) are one. 

	1. The Stage of Practice: "I am Brahman" 
	This is the stage of Sadhana (spiritual practice), where a seeker uses a Mahavakya (great saying) like "Aham Brahmasmi" (I am Brahman) to overcome ignorance. 
	Purpose: To replace the false identification with the body and ego (the "I" who suffers) with the realization of one's true, infinite nature.
	The Intent: By constantly cultivating the "pure, spiritual feeling" (Sattvic Bhava) of being Brahman, the seeker dissolves the illusion of separation (Maya). 

	2. The Final Realization: Kaivalya (Liberation)
	Kaivalya refers to the state of absolute oneness and liberation. In this state, the need for any "feeling" or "affirmation" vanishes because the truth has become self-evident. 

	Beyond Speech: The quote highlights a profound paradox: once you truly are the reality, why would you need to state it? Stating "I am Brahman" still implies a subject ("I") and an object ("Brahman"), but in Kaivalya, all such duality disappears.
	Effortless Awareness: In this state, the realization is no longer a mental thought or a repetitive mantra; it is the natural, silent, and permanent state of being where even the declaration of "I am" becomes redundant. 

	Summary of Stages
	Stage:	State of Mind:	Focus
	Practitioner (Sadhaka):		Cultivating Sattvic (Pure) thoughts:	Actively thinking "I am Brahman" to remove ego.
	Realized (Kaivalya):	Absolute Non-duality:	Silence; the realization is so total that speech is unnecessary.
	
25. In Indian philosophy, any serious spiritual or scholarly work must define its Anubandha Chatushtaya—the four foundational links that justify its study. 
	Adhikari (अधिकारी - The Seeker): The person who possesses the necessary mental purity and desire for truth to begin the journey.
	Sambandha (संबंध - The Connection): The relationship between the seeker and the goal (the 'reaper' and the 'reaped'). It defines how the knowledge connects the student to the result.
	Vishaya (विषय - The Subject Matter): The actual core topic—in this case, the practice of transforming ordinary feelings into spiritual realizations and understanding the nature of reality.
	Prayojana (प्रयोजन - The Purpose): The ultimate goal or benefit. Here, it is the lifelong, unwavering maintenance of the realization of oneness (Kaivalya). 

A Note on "Sankhya-Vedanta" 
The text describes this as "the method of Sankhya, which is the path of Vedanta." While Sankhya is often known for its dualistic focus (distinguishing between matter and spirit), in this context it refers to the analytical and knowledge-based approach (Jnana-yoga) used within Vedanta to reach non-dual realization. 

26. Virad Upasana (विराड् उपासना): This is the practice of seeing the entire universe as the physical body of the Divine. In Karma-marga, instead of withdrawing inward like a Yogi, the practitioner expands their awareness to see their own body as a microcosm of the Universal Form (Virad-rupa).
27. Knowledge vs. Meditation: Unlike Yoga, which requires intense internal focus and energy manipulation, the Path of Action focuses on Right Understanding and Sacred Duty. You "map" the worlds onto your body to sanctify your actions, viewing every physical act as a ritual offering to the cosmic whole.
28. In the Virad Upasana of the Karma-marga:
	The Feet of God: Traditionally, the Bhurloka (Earth) is described as the feet of the Cosmic Being ("pādbhyām pṛthivī").
	Humility and Service: By placing oneself at the feet, the practitioner adopts a stance of purity, humility, and service, acknowledging that the physical world is the foundation of the Divine structure.
	The Micro-Macro Link: This visualization bridges the gap between the individual "I" and the universal "Whole," turning one's presence on earth into a constant act of devotion.
29. In Virad Upasana, the practitioner visualizes the entire universe (the 14 Lokas) as the physical body of the Virat Purusha. This practice allows the seeker to move from a fragmented view of the world to a vision of divine wholeness. 

Here is how the upper and lower worlds are traditionally mapped onto the body of the Cosmic Being:

	The Seven Upper Worlds (Urdhva Lokas)
	These realms represent ascending levels of consciousness and spiritual awareness: 
	Bhur-loka (Earth): The pelvic region or hips (though often simplified as the feet in relation to the upper worlds only).
	Bhuvar-loka (Atmosphere/Space): The navel.
	Svar-loka (Heavenly Realm): The chest or heart.
	Mahar-loka (Realm of Sages): The neck or throat.
	Jana-loka (Realm of Sages/Sons of Brahma): The face or mouth.
	Tapo-loka (Realm of Penance): The forehead or space between the eyebrows.
	Satya-loka / Brahmaloka (Realm of Truth): The head or crown. 

	The Seven Lower Worlds (Patala Lokas)
	These are situated below the earthly stratum in the legs of the Virat Purusha: 
	Atala: The waist or upper thighs.
	Vitala: The thighs.
	Sutala: The knees.
	Talatala: The shanks or calves.
	Mahatala: The ankles.
	Rasatala: The heels and toes or the upper portion of the feet.
	Patala: The soles of the feet.
	
30. The Moment of Death: In Indian philosophy (Bhagavad Gita 8.6), the final thought at the moment of death determines the soul's next state. The "game" serves as a repetitive practice (Abhyasa) to ensure the mind rests on the Divine during that critical transition.
31. Samvega (संवेग - Intensity/Momentum): The text categorizes the speed of spiritual progress based on the seeker's intensity:
	Mridu (Mild): Leads to gradual spiritual progress and eventual attainment.
	Madhya (Moderate): The realization begins to manifest in the Subconscious (Dream state).
	Tivra (Intense): Leads to Direct Realization or physical attainment of the higher planes (Pratyaksha Prapti).
	
32. Yoga Vasistha's Influence: This scripture is famous for teaching that the world is a creation of the mind (Manomatram Jagat). Therefore, by changing the "sentiments" of the mind, one literally changes the reality they inhabit.

33. The Story of Queen Leela: A Case Study in Samvega
The most famous example of this in the Yoga Vasistha is the Story of Leela: 

	The Visualization: Queen Leela, through intense devotion to the goddess Saraswati, seeks to remain with her husband after his death.
	The Manifestation: Because her Samvega was so intense, she was able to mystically travel and see her husband living an entirely different life in another "world-space" created by his own last desires.
	The Lesson: Saraswati explains that the world Leela just left and the new world she sees are both just "vibrations in consciousness." What we perceive as solid reality is merely what we have intensely imagined for a long time.
34. The Role of the "Spectator" (Sakshi)
This passage introduces a crucial practical element: Accountability and Guidance.
External Oversight: Just as a sport has a referee, this spiritual "game" requires a Guru or an advanced practitioner (the Spectator) to ensure the seeker doesn't fall into delusion or "wrong imagination."
The Power of Speech: Even if you are practicing the deep, silent "Arthi" (meaningful) stage, you are told to speak it. This prevents the mind from wandering and allows the teacher to verify your progress.
35. The text references Yoga Sutra 1.38, which suggests that meditating on the insights gained during dreams or deep sleep can stabilize the mind (Chitta-prasadanam).
Sattvic Dreams: By "playing the game" (practicing the visualizations) during the day with intense focus, the subconscious mind is purified. The transition from mundane dreams to "Sattvic" or spiritual dreams is a sign of progress.
The Vyasa Bhashya: This is the primary commentary on Patanjali's Yoga Sutras. It explains that if a seeker has a dream of a divine being or a holy place and wakes up with a sense of peace and clarity, meditating on that "dream-knowledge" leads to a steady mind.
36.The Power of Consistency: Even if you can't practice daily, the text emphasizes a "rule" (Niyam) of playing once or twice a week. In spiritual practice, regularity is more important than occasional intensity.
37. A Formal Method (Prakriya): The author is elevating this "game" from a mere pastime to a formal Vedantic technique. A Prakriya is a logical, systematic method used to explain the nature of Reality (Brahman).
38. Preserving Tradition: Even while using a creative "game" format, the text insists on the Guru's presence. This ensures the seeker doesn't get lost in their own imagination or misinterpret the "sentiments."
39. The Commitment to Practice: The rule against leaving the game highlights the need for Dharana (concentration) and Nirantara Abhyasa (continuous practice). Spiritual progress is easily lost if the mind is allowed to frequently wander back to mundane tasks.
40. The "Brahma" Exception: This represents the Guru's grace or the intervention of a "superior" soul. When a student is stuck at a high spiritual plateau (the state of Brahma), the Guru can "cast the dice" for them—offering a push of energy or wisdom to help them transcend.
41. Avoiding Tamas: Tamas (darkness/inertia) is the enemy of Sattvic Bhava. Engaging in gossip, anger, or laziness during the "game" breaks the spiritual momentum and misaligns the mind from the target of Kaivalya.
42. The Final Act of Service: The mention of Paropkar at the end reflects the ideal of the Bodhisattva or Jivanmukta. Even after reaching the threshold of liberation, the highest act is to remain for a moment to help others who are still "playing the game" of life. 
43. The Upward Path: Unlike occult mirrors that pull the seeker back into visions of the material/astral world, this game uses the material as a stepping stone to reach the formless Absolute.
44. Ancient Precedent: The text identifies this "game" with established spiritual technologies like:
Sarvatobhadra Mandala: A complex, auspicious geometric design used in Vedic and Tantric worship to represent the entire cosmos.
Sacred Icons: Just as a devotee uses a physical idol to reach the formless God, the player uses the physical board to reach Kaivalya.
